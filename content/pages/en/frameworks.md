Industrial cybersecurity in Europe is no longer governed by good intentions and voluntary guidance. In the space of a few years, the EU has built an interlocking framework of laws and standards that reach directly into Operational Technology — from the operators who run critical services, to the manufacturers who build the products those services depend on, to the AI now embedded in machines. Understanding how these pieces fit together is the difference between a coherent security programme and a scramble of disconnected compliance projects.

OXOT works across all of them. This page is the map; each framework has its own detailed guide.

## How the pieces fit

The European framework divides, roughly, into **who is responsible for what**:

- **Operators** — the utilities, plants and infrastructure managers who run essential and important services — answer to **NIS2**.
- **Manufacturers** — who build products with digital elements — answer to the **Cyber Resilience Act**.
- **Machine builders** — answer to the **Machinery Regulation**, which now folds cybersecurity into machine safety.
- **Anyone deploying AI** in a safety or high-risk role answers to the **AI Act**.
- And underpinning all of it, **IEC 62443** (and, for rail, **TS 50701**) provides the engineering method to actually meet these obligations and prove it.

The laws set the outcomes; the standards provide the how. Build to the standards once, and you satisfy much of the law across the board.

## The frameworks

**[NIS2 — Directive (EU) 2022/2555](/en/nis2)**
The EU's baseline cybersecurity law for operators of essential and important services. Ten mandatory risk-management measures, strict 24/72-hour incident reporting, management accountability, and turnover-linked penalties. If you run critical industrial operations in Europe, this is your primary obligation.

**[The Cyber Resilience Act — Regulation (EU) 2024/2847](/en/cra)**
Security-by-design for products with digital elements. Manufacturers must build securable products, handle vulnerabilities, provide a support period, and report actively exploited vulnerabilities within 24 hours. Full obligations apply from December 2027; reporting duties from September 2026.

**[The AI Act — Regulation (EU) 2024/1689](/en/ai-act)**
The world's first comprehensive AI law. Its risk-based tiers land hardest on industrial AI used as a safety component — which is automatically high-risk — bringing requirements for robustness, human oversight and cybersecurity under Article 15.

**[The Machinery Regulation — Regulation (EU) 2023/1230](/en/machine-act)**
Machine safety for the connected, software and AI era. From January 2027, protection against cyber-corruption becomes part of the safety case, and AI safety components pull the AI Act in alongside.

**[IEC 62443](/en/iec-62443)**
The international standard series for industrial automation and control system security — zones and conduits, security levels, and a lifecycle covering operators, integrators and product suppliers. The engineering backbone for meeting NIS2, the CRA and the rest.

**[TS 50701](/en/ts-50701)**
The first cybersecurity specification written for railways — IEC 62443 adapted to rail applications and integrated with the EN 50126 safety lifecycle. The sector-specific method for rail operators and suppliers.

## Why it pays to treat them as one

A connected industrial product with an AI safety component, sold to an operator of an essential service, can engage **all of these at once**: the CRA for the product, the Machinery Regulation and AI Act for the machine and its AI, NIS2 for the operator, and IEC 62443 as the method threading through them. Handled separately, that is four or five overlapping compliance efforts with duplicated cost and inconsistent evidence.
