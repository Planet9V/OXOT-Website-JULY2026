## 1. Founder essay — "we've never had an incident" is not evidence

### LinkedIn post

Ten thousand fund managers flip a coin for five years. About three hundred win every year, purely by chance. They get profiled in magazines. The other 9,700 vanish without a trace — Taleb called that the cemetery.

Walk enough control rooms and you hear the same error in different words. Not "strong returns" — "we're IEC 62443 compliant." Not "a decade of returns" — "we've never had a significant incident."

A clean audit and a quiet year are not proof your OT security works. They are one sample path through a much larger space of outcomes, and most of the other paths contain a breach. The compliance score does not dip the week before the incident. The dashboard stays green right up until it doesn't.

We built the Cyber Digital Twin because we got tired of watching this mistake play out, facility after facility. Instead of "do we have the controls in place," it asks: given the full probability space of how an adversary could move through this specific plant, right now — where are we actually exposed, and by how much?

A distribution with a stated confidence interval is a more honest answer than a single score. It's also a harder one to sit with. That's the point.

## 3. NIS2 — Article 21, 24/72h reporting, management liability, €10M/2%

### LinkedIn post

NIS2 fines reach €10 million or 2% of global turnover, whichever is higher. For a €800 million group, that's €16 million. But the number that should worry a board more is Article 20: management can be held personally liable, and in serious cases, temporarily banned from management functions.

Cybersecurity just stopped being something a board delegates downward and forgets.

Article 21 sets ten minimum measures — risk analysis, incident handling, supply chain security, access control, MFA, and more — under an all-hazards approach that includes physical and environmental threats, not just hackers. Read through an OT lens, each one carries more weight than it looks: you often cannot reboot or isolate a live process the way you'd isolate a server.

Then there's the clock. Article 23 requires an early warning within 24 hours of becoming aware of a significant incident, a full notification within 72 hours, and a final report within one month. The clock starts the moment you notice — not when you finish investigating.

Most operators fail Article 21 for one reason: they cannot produce an accurate asset inventory on demand. You cannot manage risk you cannot see, and "we decided not to patch that PLC" is only defensible if it's documented.

IEC 62443 is the engineering answer to what NIS2 asks for in legal terms. Read our full field guide. [link: /en/nis2]

## 4. CRA — security by design, 24h reporting (11 Sep 2026), main obligations 11 Dec 2027, €15M/2.5%

### LinkedIn post

The CRA's reporting clock starts more than a year before its main obligations do — and it applies to products already on the market, including one sold in 2019.

From 11 September 2026, manufacturers must report actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents within 24 hours to ENISA and their national CSIRT. That's over a year before the main obligations — security by design, Annex I compliance, CE marking — apply in full on 11 December 2027.

For decades, digital products shipped with security treated as optional, and the cost landed on whoever used them. The CRA changes the deal: security becomes a legal condition of placing a product with digital elements on the EU market.

Manufacturers carry the weight: secure by design and by default, vulnerability handling, a software bill of materials, and a support period of at least five years as a rule. Importers and distributors aren't off the hook either — rebrand a product or substantially modify it, and you inherit full manufacturer obligations, contract terms notwithstanding.

Penalties reach €15 million or 2.5% of worldwide turnover, applied per non-conformant product line.

Most industrial equipment runs 15-20 years. The CRA's support floor is five. If your vendor hasn't committed to more, you're planning to run unsupported components for most of your equipment's life.

## 5. AI Act — industrial AI, the machinery safety-component = high-risk link

### LinkedIn post

An AI vision system that sorts good product from bad is minimal-risk. Wire the same system to stop a hazardous motion, and it's high-risk. Nothing about the model changed. The wiring did.

That's the line that decides whether the EU AI Act applies to the AI quietly running in your plant. Most industrial AI is not high-risk — process optimisation and maintenance forecasting that only inform a human sit in the minimal tier. The work is finding the handful of systems that cross into high-risk: Annex I (AI as a safety component of machinery needing conformity assessment) and Annex III (AI as a safety component in critical infrastructure like grid, water or gas).

Article 15 is the line OT teams should read twice: high-risk systems must prove accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity — including resistance to data poisoning and adversarial examples. In IT, a manipulated model gives a wrong answer on a screen. In OT, a manipulated model is a manipulated process.

Deadlines moved: standalone Annex III systems now apply from 2 December 2027, product-embedded Annex I AI from 2 August 2028. That's a gift of time, not a reprieve.

Penalties reach €35 million or 7% of turnover for prohibited practices; €15 million or 3% for high-risk breaches.

Start with an honest inventory of the AI already running in your plant. [link: /en/ai-act]

## 6. Machinery Regulation — cybersecurity as machine safety, 20 Jan 2027

### LinkedIn post

A guard is worthless if a forged network command retracts it. An emergency stop is worthless if its firmware can be silently overwritten. The old Machinery Directive had nothing to say about either, because in 2006 those attack surfaces barely existed.

From 20 January 2027, the EU Machinery Regulation changes the definition of a safe machine. Annex III now requires that safety cannot be compromised by corruption — accidental or intentional — of the hardware and software that affects it, and that any intervention leaves tamper evidence.

Read that precisely: it's not about protecting trade secrets. It's about ensuring corruption can't turn the machine into a hazard. A machine that can be made dangerous by tampering with its software is not merely insecure — under this Regulation, it is not compliant, and it cannot carry the CE mark.

The Regulation also names AI-based safety components directly. Deploy an AI model as a safety component in machinery, and you may simultaneously trigger the AI Act's high-risk obligations for the model itself — two regimes, one product, at the same time.

There's a trap for integrators too: "substantial modification" is now defined in law. Re-flash a safety PLC, add remote access, drop in a learning model — and you may have just become the legal manufacturer of that machine, digital changes included.

The cheap version of this project starts now. The expensive version starts in late 2026. [link: /en/machine-act]

## 7. IEC 62443 — the engineering method (zones/conduits, component security, the programme)

### LinkedIn post

NIS2 and the CRA tell you what cybersecurity outcomes to achieve. Neither tells you how. IEC 62443 is the how.

It rests on one idea that changes how you think about OT security: zones and conduits. Group assets by shared security need, control the communication paths between them, and concentrate protection where the consequence of compromise is highest — not identically everywhere.

It assigns a target security level (SL-T) per zone, from casual misuse (SL-1) up to nation-state-grade attackers (SL-4) — then separates what a component can deliver (SL-C) from what's actually achieved in the field (SL-A). The gap between target and achieved is your real remediation backlog.

It names who owns what: the asset owner runs the risk and the programme, the integrator builds the system, the supplier develops secure components. Nobody owns everything, and everyone is accountable for their part.

Map NIS2's ten Article 21 measures against IEC 62443 and they align almost item-for-item. Map CRA Annex I against 62443-4-1/4-2 and a supplier already built to the standard has done most of the engineering work.

You don't need SL-4 everywhere — that's disproportionate and expensive. You need a target set by real consequence, and a programme mature enough to sustain it.

Read the full method. [link: /en/iec-62443]

## 8. TS 50701 — railway cybersecurity built on IEC 62443

### LinkedIn post

A modern train is a data centre on wheels, running through a network the size of a country, expected to keep working safely for thirty years. For a long time, rail had no cybersecurity standard of its own — engineers borrowed generic industrial guidance and hoped the fit was close enough.

CLC/TS 50701 ended that improvisation. Published in 2021, it takes IEC 62443's zones, conduits and security levels and re-expresses them in terms a railway engineer already recognises — wired directly into the EN 50126 RAMS lifecycle they already follow.

Rail needed its own specification because of the safety-security interface. A control added for security can degrade a safety function — added latency on a safety-critical message path, a blocked diagnostic channel, an operator locked out during an emergency. A safety measure can just as easily open a security gap. TS 50701 gives that collision a formal home, including joint design reviews.

The premise underneath it all: in rail, if a system is not secure, its safety cannot be guaranteed. Security isn't adjacent to safety here — it's a precondition for it.

TS 50701 is also the on-ramp to IEC 63452, the coming international rail standard. Work done now carries forward almost unchanged.

Rail operators are essential entities under NIS2 in most member states. This is how you meet that obligation with a method built for your sector.

Read the full guide. [link: /en/ts-50701]
